LIGHT EFFECTS EXPERIENCE – GUIDE BOOK

It’s GOOD to try these:

  • Read about Hue capabilities at: developers.meethue.com
  • For atmospheric lighting use scene colors (e.g. green trees, blue sea) for the lamps near to the screen, and scene lighting (e.g. the sun) for lamps further away from the screen.
  • For special light effects (e.g. explosion, magic spells, sparkles) consider the size and location of the effect when extending it onto the lamps.
  • Light effects can be linked to the sound or to emphasize feelings (e.g. tremors, rumbles, tension building).
  • Light effects can provide information (e.g. health, powerup, off screen information, etc.).
  • Play with contrast, sometimes turning off the lamps can be as equally impactful as all on.
  • Experiment with light effects and have fun!

Please AVOID these:

  • Strobe lighting can cause epileptic symptoms; keep rapid changes in brightness to frequencies lower than 5 Hz.
  • Sudden changes in brightness of lamps in our peripheral vision (to our side) or lamps near to us, can be unpleasant.
  • Frequent transitions from very high to very low brightness, and vice versa, can also be very unpleasant; reserve this only for very special effects.

 

Please be CAUTIOUS of:

  • When translating a screen color to a lamp pay attention to the colors that different lamp types can reproduce; the risk is a mismatched of colors.
  • Slow color transitions (>1 s) can result in intermediate colors being seen.  If this is not a desired effect, avoid it by using a faster transition, or by transitioning through white (low saturation) or black (low brightness).
  • People cannot perceive colors (high saturation) well in their peripheral vision (to their sides).
  • People are very good at spotting when something is out of sync; make sure the lamps’ light effects coincide with on-screen/sound effects.
  • People will have different lamp set ups in their home.  For Hue Entertainment we recommend lamps on the left and right of the screen, with a central LED strip behind the screen.  Extended set ups may have additional lamps on the left/right, or behind, the user.

Introduction

This guidebook is intended to be a source of inspiration as well as a means of support for creating light effects that can enrich the experience of entertainment media.  Whether you are developing an application, a video game, a movie script or any other form of on-screen media, this guidebook will assist you with designing your own light effects that can be played in the room of the viewer on connected lighting systems like Hue.

For many of you, lighting may be a new topic.  For this reason, we begin this guidebook with a short introduction to lighting theory and how it can affect the human perception system.  We would also recommend taking time to familiarize yourselves with the Hue product range at http://www2.meethue.com and learn more about the Hue capabilities at http://developers.meethue.com.

In this guidebook we wanted to show you how to make your own light effects.  We have included details of how to make some of the more common light effects that occur in games and other media, such as explosions, small to large weapons fire, sparkles and flashes, and so on.  The examples include schematics for using one to multiple light sources as well as links to some lines of code that you can try.  By showing you how we created these light effects, we hope you will be able to learn from them so you can enjoy building your own for your unique media content.

General recommendations for creating light effects based on human perception of light and light dynamics

We humans perceive light in very particular ways. We do not need to cover all the details of the eye and perception system for this guidebook, but there are a few important things to consider when making light effects that extend into the room around the player/viewer.

1. Peripheral vision

This is the area of our vision that is outside the center of our gaze.

The further round something is in our peripheral vision the less color we can see; therefore, it does not make sense to make rapid color changes on the area that is in a far peripheral area of users’ vision.

Our peripheral vision helps us to detect things that are happening outside of our main area of attention.  It alerts us of things that may be happening there and so, it is very sensitive to motion or light changes.  Be cautious of how much you flash the lights that may be behind or to the sides of the viewers.  A few times can have a large effect, but if used too often it can become tiring or irritating.

2. Strobe

It is well known that strobe lighting can trigger photosensitive epilepsy.  Symptoms can occur between 5Hz to 70Hz.  Strobe can be a very impactful effect, but use it carefully by avoiding these frequencies or keeping the effect very short.  It may be necessary to warn viewers, that you include strobe effects like they do on the TV!

3. Changing color and brightness

When creating dynamic effects with transitions in color and brightness, people are more sensitive to rapid brightness changes and less sensitive to rapid color changes. Our advice is to make the brightness transition slower than the color transition.  However, sudden changes in brightness can help to maximize an effect at key moments.

4. General lighting ambiences

When creating ambiance with light based on the content, a few different approaches can be used:

  • match the colors from the screen,
  • recreate the color of light sources that illuminate the scene but are not necessarily visible on the screen,
  • create a light scene that is not based on the visible colors or light sources (e.g. associate the ambiance with a certain emotion or mood).

When implementing the first two approaches it’s better to use more saturated color from the scene on the lights that are close to the screen, and environment lighting on the lights that are closer to the user e.g. showing a green filed in a sunny day – light next to the screen could be green, while the lights next to the user could be white to represent the day light.

Examples of dynamic light effects

The Hue lighting products and the Hue Entertainment developer kit can play a wide variety of dynamic light effects that can enhance the experience of watching on-screen content.  There are essentially two categories of dynamic effect:

  1.  Effects that support on-screen light sources:
  • Sunlight
  • Explosion
  • Lightning flash
  • Weapon
  • Power-ups
  • Token collection
  • Pickups (e.g. ammunition)

  • Swirl
  • Sparkle

  1. Elements that are not necessarily visible on-screen which could support the narrative or game play, such as:

Explosions

Many different type of video games include explosions. Figures below show just a few examples of in game explosion from different type of games.

In the following sections a few of the above are described in more detail, with examples of how they can be implemented on room lighting like Hue.  Although these recommendations are largely based on our user experience studies with first person shooter games, we believe most of the advice is also relevant for any other type of game that includes the light effect that is being described.

The difference between these two example explosions show how they can vary.  The colors used can be reds and oranges with smoke or they can be white hot and intense.  The relative size of the explosions also varies based on the viewing perspective, distance from the player, location on the screen or type of explosion.

General Tips

The brightness of the light effect should be matched with the on-screen explosion.  The lights can be brighter for explosions nearby and lower brightness for explosions further away.

The orientation of the player / viewer can determine the size and brightness of the effect rendered on the lights.  For example, in a game a grenade can be thrown, and the player has time to alter their position before the explosion occurs.  How this is then rendered in the player’s room will depend on the location and number of light sources they have.  If they have no light sources behind them in the room, and they turn in the game so the in-game explosion is then behind their character, the effect should and would not be rendered on the lights.  Whereas, if they remained facing the explosion the lights in front of them in the room would render the effect.

Since the light setup of the user is known playing explosion effects on available lights irrespective of their position could be considered.  All these aspects are important to consider when designing your own explosion light effects.

Technical details

An explosion light effect is a bright flash. The characteristics of the flash depend on the in-game explosion characteristics such as color, intensity and duration. Deviations of the color of the light effect from the in-game explosion are easily noticed, especially when rendered on the lights in front of the user / near to the screen. Light effect colors should therefore match. Also, the in-game orientation of the player relative to the explosion will determine which lights the explosion effect is to be rendered. In principle, depending on orientation, explosion effects can happen all around the user.

Tips and tricks

  • Match color and duration of the effect with the color and duration of the explosion on screen
  • Match the brightness of the effect to its power, depending on relative (in-game) strength of explosions. This can depend on the in-game players distance to the explosion as well.
  • Use effect size to create an impression of explosion happening around the user, e.g. start the effect on the center light source (or light source(s) in front of the player) and then expand it to all other lights sources
  • Take into account the orientation of explosion relative to in game player position (left, right, front, rear) and render the explosion accordingly onto available lights in the room.
    • For example:
      • In game explosion left from the user à lights at the left side render the effect
      • In game explosion behind the user à  rear lights render the effect

Weapon effects

Weapons used in games vary greatly depending on the type of game.   There can be the more traditional weaponry such as fire arms and shot guns all the way to space age futuristic lasers and blasters. The light effects rendered in the room should match the weapon fire as closely as possible.

These images of different in game weapons show how the colors, brightness, firing and hitting effects differ, as does the location of the effects on the screen.   The firing characteristics also differ from single shots to constant fire and thus this level of repetition of firing rounds (speed and number) will alter how they are rendered on the lights.

Firearms

Single fire weapons fire once and then must be ‘reloaded’ after each shot.  A single shot light effect should be a ‘short bright flash’. Each shot is to be accompanied by a single light effect. The color of the effect should focus on the muzzle flash that comes out of the barrel. Its color often is (warm) white, to more ‘orange’ like for more conventional weapons.

The light effect for automatic weapons like machineguns, miniguns, but also repetitive laser weapons can be seen as a sequence of overlapping single shot light effects. As such, it is perceived as a prolonged flickering effect with a certain frequency. The ‘frequency’ of the light effect should approximate the ‘frequency’ of the shooting. It was found that a precise match of the frequency of the firing of rounds however, is not required. Much more important is that the start and the end of the light effect match the start and end of the shooting itself.  For more futuristic laser or plasma type of weapons the light effect can basically have all sorts of colors like red, green or blue, again to match with the muzzle flash or ‘laser fire’ coming out of the weapon.

Technical details

For first person shooting, the light effect is best rendered in the area around / behind the screen: directly in front of the user.  For very heavy weaponry, the region to the left and right of the screen could be considered (see optional region in figure below). We found that the size of the light effect should match with the relative size of the weapon causing the effect.  An imbalanced effect (color, size, duration etc) is easily noticeable and generally not appreciated by the user.

Tips and tricks

  • Match color of the effect with the color the muzzle flash coming out of the barrel
  • For a single shot: match duration of effect with duration of shot
  • For automatic weaponry: Match duration of effect with duration the firing (approximation of frequency of in game firing will suffice)
  • Match the brightness of the effect to its power relative to other weapons used that have similar effects. For example a pistol light effect has lower brightness than a shotgun light effect.

Suggested alternative applications

The single fire weapon type of light effect can also be used for power-ups, collection of tokens, health units or ammunition.  The flash need not be as bright, and it should take on the color of the item being picked up.  Rendering these mini moments on the lights can act as additional confirmation that the collection was successful.

The automatic weapon effect can also be tweaked to make a tremble or rumble effect.  For example, exclude the initial brightness increase and use the repetition of the smaller pitches of higher-lower brightness to give a tremble sensation.  The delta between the smaller pitches can be varied too for a more natural or random feeling effect.

Rocket Launchers

Rocket launchers typically include a launch and impact phase. In that sense they are a combination of a firearm and explosion effect. The light effect is basically a dual flash effect. Both the color as well as the intensity of the light effect may vary between launch and impact. This could be different for different games.

In some cases, the launching phase might be preceded by building up of ‘energy’ for the weapon. This is an essential part of enhancing the weapon experience and therefore should be picked up by the lights as well. In that case the light effect consists of roughly three parts, being 1) gradual increase in intensity, 2) flash during launch and 3) flash during impact.

Technical details

For first person rocket launching, the light effect is best rendered on lights around the screen.  In some cases, to stress the difference between the magnitude of the effect between launch and impact, you could consider including lights next to the screen as well for the impact phase. Tips and tricks

  • Match color of the effect with ‘fire’ during launch and during impact. Note: these are not necessarily similar.
  • Match duration of effect during launch and during impact.
  • During the ‘energy’ up phase, in case present in the in game effect, consider including a tremble effect on the light (slight increase and decreases in brightness, perhaps as the overall brightness increases).

Suggested alternative applications

This light effect can also be used to accompany magical spell casting, warp speed transitions (where the end phase may be darkness!), bumpy crash landings, etc.

Swirls & Sparkles

A swirl can be defined as ‘a light particle’ that moves in a twisting or spiraling pattern. In game content different examples exist where items such as fire flies, leaves, cards (or anything) swirl around. In many cases these kinds of swirling items also emit light. Figures below display two examples of respectively light emitting swirls and sparkles that are excellent to support with light effects.

To create a swirl light effect, the lights need to sequentially turn on an off in such a way that the user experiences the light in a spiraling fashion. Each time a separate lamp lights up (flashes) will last about 0.5 seconds, but the actual duration depends on the in-game swirl content. In a game like Journey, the swirl effect is a reoccurring effect that occurs during a pickup. It is therefore rendered several times as soon as triggered in the game.

The sparkle effect also is a series of flashing lights, but is created by rendering flashes of light across the room in a random order.

Technical details

For the swirl effect to be most effective, these are rendered on lights all around the room.

Tips and tricks

  • Match color of the light effect with color of swirl/sparkle effect in the game
  • Exact copy and mapping of the ‘physical in game model’ of the swirl/sparkles is no necessity. A general approach of speed or frequency of the on-screen effect will suffice.
  • Slight variations in the separate parts of the effect in terms of color, brightness, duration may add to the experience, especially if these are experienced on screen as well.

Other uses of light

Express information

Event information

Light effects could be used to indicate a certain state. For instance, think of Happy Hour. But in games quite often certain rules might change for a limited time: extra protection, higher speed, etc. Putting the light to a known setting (specific color) can indicate this situation. A short blinking can announce the end of the period and the light effect disappears fully at the finish.

State information

Apart from indicating a special period in time the light could also indicate a certain state. A simple yet valuable example is the health bar. One of the colored lights might be used for such an indication: green is very healthy, orange watch-it and red is you are in a danger zone. This indication can be continuously visible, but also only appear above a certain threshold. Very high danger can be showed by blinking red. Another clear level indicator could be the charge of a weapon or charging time left for a just fired weapon.

Enhance performance

Displaying event and state information with light effects could improve the performance of the player even in the case when they replicate information that is already available to the user via on screen messages or audio. For example, using the light to indicate from what side they are being attacked, or visualize the sounds of approaching enemy. The impact of such effects might diminish when the player becomes more familiar with the game so when designing those it’s important to make a good balance between ambiance/immersion effects and supportive state/event effects.

Light and board games

Light effects could be used also to enhance physical board games. The ambient light can make board games more fun. It can enhance the flow and immersion of players. By automating random elements with the help of randomly triggered thematic light effects and embedded timer feedback and keeping only the main tangible routine elements, it possible to reduced player effort and raise player enjoyment at the same time. By creating a thematic environment tightly connected to the game, it is also possible to enhanced player immersion. A companion mobile app could be used to enable such integration.
